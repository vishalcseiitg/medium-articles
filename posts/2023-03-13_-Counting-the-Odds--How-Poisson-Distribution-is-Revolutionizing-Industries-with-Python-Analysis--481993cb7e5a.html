“Counting the Odds: How Poisson Distribution is Revolutionizing Industries with Python Analysis”

The Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution used to model the number of rare, random events that occur within a fixed time interval or within a particular region. For example, the number of car accidents that occur within a city in a day, the number of calls a call center receives in an hour, or the number of defects in a production run of electronic devices.

The Poisson distribution is characterized by a single parameter denoted by the symbol λ (lambda). This parameter represents the expected number of events that occur within a fixed time interval or a particular region.

The probability mass function (PMF) of the Poisson distribution is given by:

P(X = k) = (e^(-λ) * λ^k) / k!

where:

  • P(X = k) is the probability of observing k events within the fixed time interval or region of interest.
  • e is the mathematical constant e (approximately 2.71828).
  • λ is the parameter of the Poisson distribution representing the expected number of events within the fixed time interval or region.
  • k is the number of events observed within the fixed time interval or region.
  • k! is the factorial of k.

To use the Poisson distribution, we need to know the value of λ, which represents the average rate of event occurrence within the fixed time interval or region. We can then use the PMF to calculate the probability of observing a particular number of events, such as k.

For example, suppose we know that the expected number of car accidents within a city daily is λ = 5. In that case, we can use the Poisson distribution to calculate the probability of observing a certain number of car accidents within a day. If we want to calculate the probability of observing 3 car accidents within a day, we can plug in λ = 5 and k = 3 into the PMF:

P(X = 3) = (e^(-5) * 5³) / 3! ≈ 0.1404

This tells us that the probability of observing exactly 3 car accidents within a day is approximately 0.1404, or 14.04%.

In summary, the Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution used to model the number of rare, random events that occur within a fixed time interval or within a particular region. The distribution is characterized by a single parameter λ, which represents the expected number of events. The PMF provides a way to calculate the probability of observing a particular number of events.

How the Poisson distribution is used in day-to-day life:

  1. Insurance Industry:

Insurance companies use the Poisson distribution to estimate the number of claims they are likely to receive during a given period. For example, car insurance companies use Poisson distribution to estimate the number of accidents that will occur within a particular region or time frame. This information is then used to calculate the premiums they charge their customers to cover the costs of those claims.

For instance, consider an auto insurance company that operates in a particular city. The company can use the Poisson distribution to estimate the number of car accidents that will occur in the city over the course of a year. Based on this estimate, the company can determine the premiums they need to charge their customers to cover the costs of those accidents, including the costs of repairing vehicles, providing medical care to those injured in the accidents, and covering other associated expenses.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import poisson

# Set the value of lambda
lam = 10

# Generate the PMF of the Poisson distribution
x = np.arange(0, 21)  # The range of possible values for the number of claims
pmf = poisson.pmf(x, lam)  # The probability mass function

# Plot the PMF
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(x, pmf, 'bo', ms=8)  # Plot the PMF as black circles
ax.vlines(x, 0, pmf, colors='black', lw=5)  # Draw vertical lines connecting the circles
ax.set_xlabel('Number of Claims')
ax.set_ylabel('Probability')
ax.set_title('Poisson Distribution: Average of 10 Accident Claims per Day')
plt.show()

This plot shows the probability of observing different numbers of accident claims per day in the region of interest. For example, the probability of observing 10 accident claims per day is the highest, while the probability of observing 0 or 20 accident claims per day is very low.

Using this Poisson distribution, the insurance company can estimate the probability of different numbers of accident claims and use this information to set premiums for their customers. For example, they may charge higher premiums to customers living in regions with a higher expected number of accident claims daily.

2. Telecommunications Industry:

Telecommunications companies use the Poisson distribution to estimate the number of calls they are likely to receive during a given period. This information is then used to ensure they have enough resources to handle the expected call volume.

For example, consider a call center that handles customer service inquiries for a particular company. The call center can use the Poisson distribution to estimate the number of calls they receive during a particular day, week, or month. Based on this estimate, they can ensure enough staff to handle the expected call volume and avoid long wait times or missed calls.

Let’s assume that a call center receives an average of 100 calls per hour during peak hours. We can model this using the Poisson distribution with λ = 100/60 = 1.67 calls per minute. ax.set_ylabel('Probability')

plt.show()

This plot shows the probability of observing different numbers of calls per minute during peak hours. For example, the probability of observing 1 call per minute is the highest, while the probability of observing 0 or 5 or more calls per minute is very low.

Using this Poisson distribution, the call center can estimate the probability of different numbers of calls and use this information to ensure they have enough staff on hand to handle the expected call volume. For example, they may schedule more staff during peak hours when the expected call volume is higher.
